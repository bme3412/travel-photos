Some cities hide their history; Berlin leaves it in the open. Two days at the end
of June in Germany's capital — a place that wears the twentieth century on its
surface and keeps building over the top of it, the Brandenburg Gate and the last
of the Wall and a restless modern pulse all crowded into one short midsummer stay.

<Figure src="IMG_0171.jpg" caption="Germany's capital, held in close focus over two midsummer days." />

## History in plain sight

Berlin doesn't cordon off its past. Remnants of the Wall run straight through the
ordinary city, monuments and memorials set among working streets, so that a walk
here is also a walk through recent history. The weight of it is everywhere and
nowhere in particular — embedded in the everyday rather than roped off from it.

## Gates and monuments

At the civic heart stand the great set pieces — the Brandenburg Gate first among
them, boulevards and squares opening out around it. These are the postcards, but
standing among them the scale registers differently: the weight of a capital that
has been the center of so much, gathered into a few blocks of stone and open sky.

<Gallery srcs={["IMG_0194.jpg", "IMG_0199.jpg", "IMG_0202.jpg", "IMG_0205.jpg", "IMG_0212.jpg", "IMG_0217.jpg"]} caption="The civic set pieces at the center of the city." />

## A city still in motion

Beyond the monuments runs the restless present — the arts, the streets, the sense
of a place perpetually remaking itself. Berlin never quite settles, and the days
took on that momentum, moving between the historic and the brand-new without any
seam between them.

Two days in a capital that holds its history and its reinvention in the same hand.
