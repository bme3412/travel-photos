Menton is where France runs out. The Riviera keeps going east — Nice, Monaco,
Roquebrune — until it reaches this one last town, and then the border, and then
Italy. Two days at the end of June were enough to feel the whole of it: a place
that faces the sun full-on, stacked in warm colour above a bright stretch of the
Mediterranean.

<Figure src="IMG_0343.jpg" caption="The last French town on the coast, holding the morning light." />

## The old town, in tiers

The heart of Menton climbs. Ochre and rose and faded yellow façades rise in
tiers up the hillside, narrow stepped lanes threading between them toward the
Baroque churches at the top. It is a town built to be walked slowly and looked
up at — every turn offering another wall of sun-warmed stucco, another glimpse
of shutters and washing lines and the sea beyond.

## Lemon light

This is lemon country — the fruit that gives the town its emblem and its
famous winter festival grows on these slopes year-round. Even in June the light
seems to carry it: a particular clarity coming off the water and the pale stone,
warm on the terraces and gardens that the Belle Époque left scattered across the
hills above the centre.

## The water

And then the sea, always just below. The old town gives onto a long curve of
Mediterranean — the port and its boats at one end, the pebble beach running out
toward the frontier at the other, Italy visible along the coast. The days
settled into the easy rhythm of a town with nowhere else to be: the walk down to
the front, the light shifting on the water through the afternoon.

Two unhurried days on the edge of France, the border just past the last houses
and the Mediterranean spread out below the whole time.
