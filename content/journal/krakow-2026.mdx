Kraków is one of the few great cities of the region to have come through the war
intact — Poland's old royal capital, left largely whole where so much around it
was lost. Three days at the end of June, no onward leg and no contrast of
landscapes, just the slow, deliberate work of getting to know one medieval city.

<Figure src="IMG_0269.jpg" caption="Poland's old royal capital, given three unhurried days." />

## The Rynek Główny

The days turned around the Rynek Główny, the vast market square at the center of
the old town — one of the largest in Europe, ringed by townhouses and arcades with
the Cloth Hall standing in its middle. It is the kind of space a whole city
organizes itself around; every walk seemed to begin or end here.

## Wawel, above the Vistula

Above the river rose Wawel — the castle and cathedral on their hill, the old seat
of Polish kings, the Vistula moving below. From up there the city laid itself out:
red roofs and church towers, the medieval plan still legible under the present-day
life running through it.

<Gallery srcs={["IMG_0301.jpg", "IMG_0306.jpg", "IMG_0310.jpg", "IMG_0313.jpg", "IMG_0316.jpg", "IMG_0322.jpg"]} caption="The castle hill and the river below." />

## An old city, whole

And then the streets themselves — the churches, the gates, the stones of a royal
city that history mostly spared. Kraków rewards slow walking: turning corners into
small squares, the past close enough to touch, a place that asks to be lingered in
rather than passed through.

Three unhurried days in a medieval capital, given the full attention it deserves.
