Some places you arrive at already knowing their silhouette. Egypt is one of
them — the pyramids are lodged in the imagination long before you stand at
their base and realise how much *ground* they cover, how the desert begins
exactly where the last suburb of Cairo ends.

<Figure src="IMG_1669.jpg" caption="A camel at the edge of the Giza plateau, the city at its back." />

## Cairo

The first days belonged to the old capital, where the ancient and the modern
press right up against each other. You can photograph a five-thousand-year-old
tomb with a traffic jam in the same frame. The pyramids are the obvious draw,
but it's the scale of everything around them — the sprawl, the noise, the
sheer density of history stacked on history — that stays with you.

<PullQuote>
The desert begins exactly where the last suburb ends.
</PullQuote>

<Gallery srcs={["IMG_1646.jpg", "IMG_1652.jpg", "IMG_1696 3.jpg"]} caption="Giza, morning to midday." />

## Luxor

Then south, to the old kingdom of Thebes. If Cairo is about silhouettes, Luxor
is about interiors — the Valley of the Kings, the great hypostyle hall at
Karnak, walls dense with hieroglyphs and colour that has survived three
thousand years. This was the richer chapter by far, the one that rewards a
slower look.

<Figure src="IMG_1930 2.jpg" caption="Carved detail, still legible after three millennia." wide />

Two cities, one river, and the long weight of history running between them.
